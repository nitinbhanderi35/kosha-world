It is perfectly possible that 28 is the earlier number, and that Abhijit dropped out because it was faint, or too far north, or because 27 was a more mystic (3×3×3) number: it is significant that the Chinese Sieou and the Arabic Manāzil are 28 in number.<sup>28</sup> Weber,<sup>29</sup> however, believes that 27 is the older number in India. <dvsn n='P'/>The meaning of the number is easily explained when it is remembered that a periodic month occupies something between 27 and 28 days, more nearly the former number. Such a month is in fact recognized in the Lāṭyāyana<sup>30</sup> and Nidāna Sūtras<sup>31</sup> as consisting of 27 days, 12 months making a year of 324 days, a Nakṣatra year, or with an intercalary month, a year of 351 days. The Nidāna Sūtra<sup>32</sup> makes an attempt to introduce the Nakṣatra reckoning into the civil or solar (<i>sāvana</i>) year of 360 days, for it holds that the sun spends 13(1/3) days in each Nakṣatra (13(1/3)×27 = 360). But the month of 27 or 28 days plays no part in the chronological calculations of the Veda.<sup>33</sup> <dvsn n='P'/><i>The Names of the Nakṣatras.</i> — In addition to the two mentioned in the Rigveda, the earlier Atharvaveda<sup>34</sup> gives the names of Jyeṣṭhaghnī<sup>35</sup> (the later Jyeṣṭhā) and Vicṛtau,<sup>36</sup> which are mentioned as in close connexion, and of Revatīs (plural) and Kṛttikās.<sup>37</sup> With reference to possible times for the ceremony of the Agnyādhāna, or ‘laying of the sacred fires,’ the Kāṭhaka Saṃhitā,<sup>38</sup> the Maitrāyaṇī Saṃhitā,<sup>39</sup> and the Taittirīya Brāhmaṇa<sup>40</sup> mention the Nakṣatras called Kṛttikās, Rohiṇī, Phalgunyas, Hasta; the latter Brāhmaṇa adds Punarvasū, and in an additional remark<sup>41</sup> excludes Pūrve Phalgunī in favour of Uttare Phalgunī.
