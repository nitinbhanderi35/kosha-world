But the most frequent use of ‘in’ is expressed in Sanskrit by the locative case, without any preposition; as, ‘in the city,’ नगरे; ‘in the night,’ रात्रौ; ‘a tree planted in the earth,’ पृथिव्यां रोपितो वृक्षः; ‘confidence in you,’ त्वयि विश्वासः; ‘engaging in the acquisition of wealth,’ अर्थार्जने प्रवृत्तिः <i>f.;</i> ‘the sun in the east,’ प्राचि सूर्य्यः; ‘he sinks in the mud,’ पङ्के मज्जति; ‘he delights in the good of others,’ परहिते रमते; ‘in the front of the fight,’ रणमूर्द्धि. In cases where ‘in’ refers to any division of time, or the manner in which any thing is done, it is usually expressed in Sanskrit by the instr. case; as, ‘in three years,’ त्रिभिर् वर्षैः; ‘in twelve months,’ द्वादशभिर् मासैः; ‘in hundreds of years,’ वर्षशतैः; ‘in an instant,’ क्षणेन; ‘in process of time,’ कालपर्य्यायेण; ‘in how long time?’ कियता कालेन; ‘in a short time,’ अचिरेण; ‘in this manner,’ अनेन प्रकारेण; ‘in abundance,’ बाहुल्येन; ‘in the form of a man,’ नररूपेण; ‘in joke,’ परिहासेन; ‘written in Sanskrit,’ संस्कृतेन रचितं.
