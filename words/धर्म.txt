On the other hand, the Dharma Sūtras<sup>3</sup> contain full particulars. <dvsn n='P'/>(1) <i>Criminal Law.</i> — The crimes recognized in Vedic literature vary greatly in importance, while there is no distinction adopted in principle between real crimes and what now are regarded as fanciful bodily defects or infringements of merely conventional practices.<sup>4</sup> The crimes enumerated include the slaying of an embryo (<i>bhrūṇa</i>),<sup>5</sup> the slaying of a man (<i>vīra</i>),<sup>6</sup> and the slaying of a Brahmin,<sup>7</sup> a much more serious crime. Treachery is mentioned in the Pañcaviṃśa Brāhmaṇa<sup>8</sup> as being punishable by death, as it was punished later.<sup>9</sup> But there is no trace of an organized criminal justice vested either in the king or in the people. There still seems to have prevailed the system of wergeld (<b>Vaira</b>), which indicates that criminal justice remained in the hands of those who were wronged. In the Sūtras,<sup>10</sup> on the other hand, the king's peace is recognized as infringed by crimes, a penalty being paid to him, or, according to the Brahminical textbooks, to the Brahmins. It may therefore reasonably be conjectured that the royal power of jurisdiction steadily increased; the references in the Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa to the king as wielding punishment (<b>Daṇḍa</b>) confirm this supposition. Whether, as the analogy of other systems suggests, the king was assisted in his judicial duties, as he undoubtedly was later, by assessors, presumably of the Brahmin caste, cannot be made out clearly.<sup>11</sup> <dvsn n='P'/>The procedure adopted in deciding cases is quite uncertain. In the Chāndogya Upaniṣad<sup>12</sup> the ordeal of the red-hot axe is mentioned as applied in an accusation of theft. It must apparently be understood to have been inflicted by the direction of the king. But no other judicial ordeal is known to Vedic literature (see <b>Divya</b>). The punishment of theft was in some cases at least death, probably when the thief was taken redhanded;<sup>13</sup> in other cases binding to posts was the penalty,<sup>14</sup> presumably accompanied by the return of the stolen goods. In the Chāndogya Upaniṣad<sup>15</sup> the list of sins given as apparently equal in wickedness is stealing gold, drinking spirits, defiling a Guru's bed, and the murder of a Brahmin. <dvsn n='P'/>(2) <i>Civil Law.</i> — There is little recorded as to civil law in Vedic literature. The relations of the family and the question of family property are dealt with under <b>Urvarā, Kṣetra, Pati;</b> succession and partition of property are treated under <b>Dāya.</b> As regards the transfer of chattels — for land as transferable <i>inter vivos</i> is hardly yet recognized, save exceptionally as a sacrificial fee (<b>Dakṣiṇā</b>), and then disapproved<sup>16</sup> — the recognized modes are gift (<b>Dāna</b>) and barter or sale (<b>Kraya</b>), which includes exchange. Original acquisition of land was no doubt brought about by occupation and apportionment among the tribesmen,<sup>17</sup> while chattels were acquired by taking possession of them, provided that they were found on one's own land or on unoccupied land, and did not belong to any other person originally. The Sūtras<sup>18</sup> contain rules for the disposal of lost property, which tend to give it to the king, with the deduction of a percentage for the finder, unless the latter is a Brahmin, who keeps it all. As for contract, save in regard to moneylending (for which see <b>Ṛṇa</b>), practically nothing is to be gathered from Vedic literature, doubtless because of the primitive conditions prevailing in that early period. Much of the labour, which would in a more developed society have been done by workers for hire, would be performed by slaves (<i>cf.</i> <b>Dāsa,</b> <b>Śūdra</b>), while the technical workers of the village — of whom long lists are given in the Vājasaneyi Saṃhitā<sup>19</sup> and the Taittirīya Brāhmaṇa<sup>20</sup> — may have been recompensed not by any sum based on each piece of work done, but by fixed allowances, much as the village servants are in modern times.<sup>21</sup> But this must remain a matter of conjecture, and it is uncertain what exact status the carpenter or smith held in the village. Similarly it is impossible to trace in the early literature any legal theory or practice as to torts, but rules as to penalties for insults appear in the Sūtras.<sup>22</sup> <dvsn n='P'/>Very little is recorded as to procedure. The same idea may be conveyed by the word <i>madhyama-śī,</i> ‘lying in the midst,’ which occurs in the Rigveda,<sup>24</sup> and which Roth,<sup>25</sup> followed by Zimmer,<sup>26</sup> understood to mean an arbitrator or judge, the expression being derived from the judge acting with other judicial persons,<sup>27</sup> and being surrounded by the assembly of the people. But this interpretation is uncertain; Whitney<sup>28</sup> thinks that the word merely alludes to a chief round whom his men encamp. The king is later the chief civil judge, and may presumably have been so earlier, no doubt in conjunction with the elders of the tribe, but for this we are reduced to conjecture.<sup>29</sup> <dvsn n='P'/>The use of witnesses as evidence is uncertain (see <b>Jñātṛ</b>), and the ordeal is not recorded as deciding any civil matter except the dispute between <b>Vatsa</b> and his rival as to the true Brahminical descent of the former, which was settled by his walking unharmed through the flame of a fire.<sup>30</sup> But it is probable on analogy that the ordeal may have been used for the purpose of deciding disputes.
